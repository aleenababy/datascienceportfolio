The problem

Molecular clouds are the birth place of stars in the Galaxy When a star forms it will irradiate the clouds nearby. PDRs are regions where interstellar gas and dust are exposed to intense ultraviolet radiation, often near massive stars. Physics and chemistry of this regions will be influenced by the radiation from the star. Understanding the physical and chemical processes within these regions is crucial for astrophysical research. The transport of molecules, atoms, ions, energy, or momentum influence the chemistry and physics of the medium. Quantifying this transport is essential for understanding the underlying physical conditions in a molecular cloud.

Objectives

  1. Incorporate a systematic diffusion model into the KOSMA-tau PDR model.

    2. The KOSMA-tau PDR model is an advanced numerical code meticulously crafted for unraveling the complexities of physics and chemistry within photodissociation regions, particularly in spherical geometry. Crafted using the FORTRAN programming language, this model boasts an adaptive mesh that empowers it to navigate the nuances of the region with precision, adjusting resolution as required.

    While the adaptive mesh brings significant benefits, a notable limitation arises: the position of the subsequent shell is only considered post-solving the current shell. This sequential consideration poses a challenge when incorporating diffusion into the model. To accurately model diffusion, it becomes imperative to possess information about the position, density, and temperature of neighboring shells. Achieving this requires a thoughtful approach that harmonizes the adaptive mesh framework with the demands of incorporating diffusion chemistry into the model.

    To solve this problem, I have created an interpolation technique which determines the position, density and temperature of the next shell. Then the diffusion rates are calculated using the Lagrange's three-point interpolation
